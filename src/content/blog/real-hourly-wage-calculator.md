Quick question: how much do you earn per hour?

If you said something like "$30" or "£22" -- you are almost certainly wrong. And the gap between your assumed hourly rate and your **real** hourly rate might be the most important number in your financial life that you have never calculated.

## The Number Everyone Gets Wrong

Here is what most people do. They take their annual salary, divide by 2,080 (40 hours times 52 weeks), and announce the result as their hourly rate. A $60,000 salary becomes $28.85 an hour. A £40,000 salary becomes £19.23 an hour.

Simple. Clean. **And wildly inaccurate.**

That calculation ignores taxes, commuting costs, work wardrobe expenses, the lunch you buy because you are too tired to meal prep, the decompression wine after a rough day, the childcare that exists solely because you work, and the unpaid hours you spend answering emails at 9 PM.

When Vicki Robin and Joe Dominguez introduced this concept in *Your Money or Your Life*, they called money "life energy." The idea is simple but devastating: every dollar you spend was earned with a piece of your irreplaceable time on earth. So you had better know exactly how much time each dollar actually cost you to earn.

## How to Calculate Your Real Hourly Wage

This takes about 15 minutes and you only need to do it once (or whenever your job changes). Grab a pen or open a spreadsheet.

### Step 1: Start with your gross income

Your annual salary or total earnings before any deductions. If your income varies, use the last 12 months.

### Step 2: Subtract taxes

Income tax, national insurance, social security, Medicare, state/local taxes -- everything that gets taken before you see your money. Check your last payslip for the exact numbers.

### Step 3: Subtract work-related costs

This is where most people are shocked. Add up everything you spend *because* you work:

| Work expense | Monthly estimate | Annual cost |
|---|---|---|
| Commuting (fuel, transit, parking) | $250 | $3,000 |
| Work lunches and coffees | $150 | $1,800 |
| Professional wardrobe | $75 | $900 |
| Childcare (work hours portion) | $400 | $4,800 |
| After-work decompression (takeaway, drinks) | $100 | $1,200 |
| Professional tools/subscriptions | $30 | $360 |
| Convenience services (cleaners, meal kits) | $80 | $960 |
| **Total work costs** | **$1,085** | **$13,020** |

That is over $13,000 a year you spend just to maintain the ability to work. Money that would not leave your account if you did not have this particular job.

### Step 4: Calculate your net earnings

> Net earnings = Gross salary - Taxes - Work expenses

For a $60,000 salary with $15,000 in taxes and $13,000 in work costs:

> $60,000 - $15,000 - $13,000 = **$32,000**

### Step 5: Count your real work hours

It is not 40 hours a week. Count everything:

| Time category | Hours/week |
|---|---|
| Contracted hours | 40 |
| Commute (round trip) | 5 |
| Getting ready for work | 2.5 |
| Unpaid overtime/emails | 3 |
| Work-related errands | 1 |
| Recovery and decompression | 2.5 |
| **Total** | **54** |

Over 50 working weeks, that is 2,700 hours -- not 2,000.

### Step 6: Divide

> Real hourly wage = Net earnings / Real hours

> $32,000 / 2,700 = **$11.85 per hour**

:::end

That $28.85 you thought you were earning? It is actually $11.85. Less than half.

Take a moment to sit with that. Then read on, because this number is about to transform how you spend money.

## What Your Real Hourly Wage Reveals About Your Spending

Once you know your real rate, every price tag becomes a time tag.

### The everyday math

| Purchase | Price | At $28.85/hr (fake) | At $11.85/hr (real) |
|----------|-------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Takeaway dinner | $35 | 1.2 hours | 3.0 hours |
| New shoes | $150 | 5.2 hours | 12.7 hours |
| Weekend trip | $400 | 13.9 hours | 33.8 hours |
| New laptop | $1,200 | 41.6 hours | 101.3 hours |

That $1,200 laptop is not a week of work. It is **101 hours** of your actual life energy -- almost three full work weeks when measured honestly.

:::takeaway label="The Shift"
When you use your real hourly wage instead of your assumed rate, the true cost of every purchase roughly doubles. That is not pessimism -- it is accuracy. And accuracy is what prevents regret.
:::end

## Why This Changes Everything About How You Shop

Knowing your real hourly wage does three powerful things:

### 1. It kills impulse purchases instantly

When your brain says "it's only $50," your new brain replies "that's four hours of my life." Four hours of sitting at a desk, dealing with a difficult client, missing your kid's football match. Suddenly, the impulse dies on the spot.

### 2. It makes quality investments obvious

A $300 winter coat that lasts five years and gets worn 400 times costs you 25 hours of life energy but only $0.75 per wear. A $60 coat that lasts one season and gets worn 30 times costs 5 hours of life energy but $2.00 per wear -- and you have to repeat the purchase every year.

The real hourly wage makes the quality investment **obviously** correct, because you can see the total life energy over time, not just the upfront trade.

### 3. It reveals your fulfillment-per-hour ratio

Some purchases genuinely make your life better in proportion to the hours they cost. A $200 espresso machine used daily for three years costs about 17 hours of life energy but delivers over 1,000 moments of genuine pleasure. That is an incredible return.

Other purchases -- the gadget drawer, the forgotten subscription, the sale item that never fit -- cost hours of life energy and return nothing.

## How to Use This Number Every Day

You do not need to redo the full calculation for every purchase. Here is the daily system:

1. **Save your real hourly rate** in your phone's notes or calculator
2. **Before buying anything over $20**, mentally divide by your rate
3. **Ask yourself**: "Would I work X hours specifically for this?"
4. **For bigger purchases**, combine with cost per use -- divide the time cost by expected uses to see the life energy per use

:::checklist
- [ ] Gather your last 3 payslips and tax summaries
- [ ] List every work-related expense for the last month
- [ ] Calculate your total weekly hours including commute and prep time
- [ ] Run the formula: (gross - tax - work expenses) / total real hours
- [ ] Save the number and start using it before your next purchase
:::end

## Your Time Is the Only Thing You Cannot Get Back

Money comes and goes. You can earn more tomorrow. You can get a raise, a bonus, a side gig. But the hours you traded for that money? Those are gone permanently.

Knowing your real hourly wage is not about feeling bad. It is about feeling *informed*. It is about making sure that when you exchange your life energy for something, that something is genuinely worth it.

Most purchases are not.

The good ones are worth every minute.
