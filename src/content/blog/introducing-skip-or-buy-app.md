## The Hidden Truth About What Things Cost

The price tag on an item tells you almost nothing about its true value. A $200 pair of boots you wear 300 times over three years costs you $0.67 per wear. A $50 pair of trendy shoes you wear five times before they fall apart costs you $10 per wear. The "cheap" option was actually **15 times more expensive** in practice.

This idea is called **cost per use**, and it is the single most powerful concept in smart spending. The formula is simple:

**Cost Per Use = Purchase Price / Number of Times You Use It**

## Why We Built This

The numbers around impulse spending are staggering. Studies consistently show that the average person spends over $5,000 per year on impulse purchases, and roughly 70% of those purchases are later regretted. That is thousands of dollars drained from savings accounts, retirement funds, and experiences that would have brought lasting happiness.

The problem is not that people are irresponsible. The problem is that modern retail -- both online and in stores -- is specifically engineered to bypass rational decision-making. Flash sales create artificial urgency. "Only 2 left in stock" triggers scarcity fear. Free shipping thresholds push you to add items you did not need. One-click purchasing removes every friction point between impulse and action.

Against all of that psychological machinery, what do you have? Willpower? A vague sense that you "should not spend too much"?

We built Skip or Buy because we believe people deserve a **tool** that is as sophisticated as the forces working against them. Not a budgeting app that makes you feel guilty. Not a spreadsheet that takes 20 minutes to update. A fast, beautiful, intelligent tool that gives you the one number that matters at the exact moment you need it.

## The Real Cost of Not Knowing

Let us make this concrete. Imagine you are considering three purchases this month:

1. **A $120 fitness tracker** you will wear daily for 2 years -- that is $0.16 per use. Probably worth it.
2. **A $75 kitchen gadget** you will use twice a month for a year -- that is $3.13 per use. Think carefully.
3. **A $45 novelty item** you will use three times and forget about -- that is $15.00 per use. Skip it.

Without running those numbers, all three feel like reasonable, modest purchases. Together they total $240 -- not a huge amount. But two of those three will end up being poor value, wasting $120 on items that fail the cost per use test.

Now multiply that pattern across a year. Across a decade. The difference between someone who evaluates purchases this way and someone who does not can easily reach **tens of thousands of dollars**.
